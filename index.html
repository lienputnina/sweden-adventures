Learning Swedish

Swedish is a North Germanic language. North Germanic languages make up one of the three branches of the Germanic languages. They are often divided into Continental Scandinavian languages (Swedish, Danish and Norwegian) and Insular Nordic languages (Faroese, Icelandic, Norn and Greenlandic Norse).

Swedish is the official language of Sweden. Safety instructions and product information must be available in Swedish. The language used in schools should also normally be Swedish. It is spoken by almost 10 million people. Around 9 million people are native Swedish speakers, of which 8.5 million live in Sweden.

Until World War II Swedish was spoken in Estonia and Latvia as well. The latter were part of the Swedish Livonia during the 17th-18th century (Plakans, 2011). Historical ties explain, why there are quite a few Latvian words spoken and written almost exactly the same as in Swedish. Below are some examples: A pan: Swedish – ‘panna’ (also means ‘forehead’), Latvian – panna A chimney: Swedish – ‘skorsten’, Latvian – skurstenis A school: Swedish – skola, Latvian – skola A screw: Swedish – skruv, Latvian – skrūve, etc. There are also funny examples, where the word has a completely different meaning, like: Pumpa – ‘a pumpkin’ in Swedish, ‘a pimple’ in Latvian. ‘Pumpafro’ means pumpkin seeds. Imagine my amusement, walking into a store here on my first week in Sweden.
